# Website Redesign Brief: compute.regen.network

## What This Is

Regen Compute is a subscription service that connects AI usage to verified ecological credit retirement on Regen Network, a public blockchain. Users subscribe ($1.25-$5/month), and their payments fund permanent retirement of ecological credits from real projects around the world. Every retirement is recorded on-chain -- publicly auditable, not a private database. There's also an MCP plugin for AI coding tools (Claude Code, Cursor) that gives the AI ecological awareness inside the developer workflow.

The current site is at https://compute.regen.network. We need a redesign that makes visitors *feel* the projects they'd be funding -- not just understand the product intellectually.

## Who Visits This Site

Three primary audiences, in order of volume:

1. **AI users who care about doing the right thing** -- They already use AI daily. They're not looking for permission to use more. They want a credible, easy way to take responsibility for the footprint they already have. They're skeptical of greenwashing. They respond to honesty, specificity, and proof. Price is not the barrier ($1.25/mo) -- conviction is.

2. **Developers** -- They found us through the MCP plugin, Claude Code community, or GitHub. They want to see the tech is real (open source, on-chain verification). They might subscribe after using the free tools.

3. **ReFi / blockchain-ecology community** -- Already understand Regen Network. They want to see how this drives credit demand and REGEN token burns. Smallest audience but most vocal advocates.

## The Core Emotional Job

The site's job is NOT to explain a SaaS product. It's to make someone think: **"These are real places. Real communities. Real ecosystems. I want to be part of funding this."**

The subscription is just the mechanism. The *feeling* should be: I'm connected to something beautiful and meaningful that's happening on the ground, in real landscapes, with real people. My $1.25/month matters because it flows to *this specific forest in Colombia*, *this jaguar habitat in Ecuador*, *this farm in Napa Valley*.

Right now, most "climate tech" sites are cold, corporate, dashboard-heavy. We want the opposite. We want warmth, specificity, and a sense of place.

## Voice and Tone

### We sound like:
- A friend who works in ecology telling you about something real they built
- Honest, warm, specific, grounded
- Confident without being salesy -- we believe in what we're doing and we show the proof
- Direct about what this is and what it isn't

### We never sound like:
- A SaaS landing page ("Unlock your potential! Start your free trial!")
- A guilt trip ("Your AI is destroying the planet")
- Corporate sustainability ("Committed to net-zero by 2030")
- Vague or aspirational ("Making the world a better place")

### Key language rules:
- Say "regenerative contribution" -- never "carbon offset" or "carbon neutral"
- Say "on-chain proof" and "publicly auditable" -- this is our differentiator
- Say "priced at actual impact cost" -- we did the math, this is what it costs
- Say "approximate footprint" when discussing estimates -- never claim precision
- Frame as action, not absolution: "You did something. Here's the evidence."
- Never imply that using more AI is good because of this product
- Never use shame or guilt -- the tone is agency and accountability

### The one-liner:
"Every AI session uses energy. Fund verified ecological regeneration alongside your usage. On-chain proof. Plans from $1.25/month."

## Design Direction

### Overall feel:
- **Warm, not cold.** Think National Geographic meets indie web, not Stripe or Linear.
- **Photography-forward.** The project images should be large, vivid, immersive. Let the landscapes do the talking.
- **Earthy, natural palette.** Greens, warm earth tones, muted blues. Not neon tech gradients.
- **Sense of place.** The visitor should feel like they're looking at real places on Earth, not abstract concepts.
- **Typography that breathes.** Generous whitespace. Let the content land. Don't crowd it.

### What to avoid:
- Dashboard screenshots as the hero (save those for after signup)
- Abstract illustrations of "the planet" or generic leaf/globe icons
- Dark mode tech aesthetic (we're not a dev tool landing page)
- Busy layouts with too many CTAs competing for attention
- Stock photography -- use only the real project images

### Page structure suggestion (adapt as you see fit):

1. **Hero** -- One powerful project image (full-bleed or near-full). A single sentence: what this is and why it matters. Subscribe CTA.

2. **The Projects** -- This is the emotional core. Each project as an immersive card or section: photo, location, one-paragraph story, credit type badge. The visitor should scroll through these and feel: "I want to fund this." Link each to its Regen Network project page for full details.

3. **How It Works** -- Simple, clear, 3-step: Subscribe -> We retire credits from real projects -> You get on-chain proof. Keep it brief.

4. **Pricing** -- $1.25 / $2.50 / $5 per month (or yearly at 17% off). Emphasize: "Priced at actual impact cost." Don't oversell tiers -- the difference is just how many sessions you cover.

5. **The Proof** -- On-chain verification. Link to Regen Ledger. Maybe show a sample retirement certificate. Emphasize: this isn't a marketing claim, it's a public record.

6. **For Developers** -- Smaller section: MCP plugin, one-command install, open source on GitHub. This audience already knows they want it; just show them how.

7. **Footer** -- Links: GitHub, npm, X (@Regen_compute), Regen Network. "Open source. Apache-2.0."

## Technical Context

- The site is server-rendered from a Node.js/Express server (the same server that runs the API and dashboard)
- Landing page HTML is generated in `src/server/dashboard.ts` or served as static HTML
- Project images are served from `/projects/` path (optimized JPGs already exist)
- The dashboard (post-login) is a separate concern -- this brief is about the public-facing landing page only
- The site supports 22 languages via a translations system (language picker in header)
- There is an existing Stripe checkout integration for subscriptions

## What Success Looks Like

A visitor arrives. They see a real landscape -- maybe the Colombian cloud forest or the Napa Valley vineyard with sheep. They read one sentence and understand what this is. They scroll and encounter each project like a story: here is a place, here is what's happening there, here is how your subscription funds it. They feel connected to something tangible. The price is so low it's a non-decision. They subscribe because they *want to be part of this*, not because they were sold on features.

The site should feel like an invitation to participate in something beautiful, not a transaction.
